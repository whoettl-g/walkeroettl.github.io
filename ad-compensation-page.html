Athletic Director Compensation Modeling

Undergraduate Capstone Thesis | Spring 2025

R Linear Regression Data Imputation HAC Standard Errors

Overview

Athletic Directors are the pseudo-CEOs of college sports—managing multimillion-dollar budgets, coaching transitions, and the broader success of athletic programs. Yet unlike coaches, whose salaries are widely reported and studied, AD compensation remains largely opaque. This thesis attempts to model the determinants of AD pay across NCAA Division I programs.

Building on Marburger's (2015) foundational work, this study extends the analysis from 2015-2023, incorporating department revenues, recruiting success (football and basketball), NACDA Directors' Cup performance, and conference affiliation as predictors.

Data Collection

The core challenge of this project was data availability. Unlike coaches, AD salaries aren't published in accessible databases. I assembled compensation data from IRS Form 990 filings (Schedule J) for public universities, then built a predictive model to impute missing values using the NCAA Membership Financial Reporting System's administrative compensation category.

Additional data came from 247sports.com for recruiting rankings, NACDA for Directors' Cup points, and NCAA Membership reports for revenue and conference affiliation. The final dataset spans 1,400+ school-year observations across Division I programs.

Distribution Analysis

Relationship between IRS 990 compensation and NCAA administrative spending—the basis for imputation.

Initial distribution showed multimodality and right skew—common when mixing programs of vastly different scales.

Faceting by division (FBS, FCS, No Football) resolved some issues, but FBS still showed multimodality due to Power 5 vs. Group of 5 differences.

Updating these facets with Power 5 vs. Group of 5 differences shows the most unimodal distributions and also highlights that most upper ouliers exist in the Power 5 conferences.

Methodology

The model predicts administrative compensation as a function of department revenues, Directors' Cup points, football and basketball recruiting scores, year, and a categorical variable for division/conference tier (Power 5, Group of 5, FCS, No Football). I also built a lagged version to examine how prior-year performance influences following-year compensation decisions.

Diagnostic testing revealed heteroscedasticity and autocorrelation in residuals. To address this, I applied Heteroscedasticity and Autocorrelation Consistent (HAC) standard errors, which increased standard errors but provided valid inference.

Key Findings

Power 5 membership dominates. Conference affiliation had the largest effect on compensation, reflecting the financial advantages of enhanced branding, TV deals, and larger budgets at elite programs.

Revenues matter. Department revenues consistently emerged as a significant predictor across all model specifications.

Upward trend over time. The year variable was persistently significant, reflecting the broader increase in spending on college athletics administration.

Football recruiting effects are complex. Interestingly, football recruiting showed a negative association in the base model but positive (non-significant) in the lagged model—possibly because strong recruiting classes often follow weaker seasons.

Challenges & Limitations

This project was a lesson in working with imperfect data. The reliance on imputed compensation values, while necessary, introduces uncertainty. A more complete dataset with actual base salaries, bonuses, and contract details would strengthen the analysis. Multicollinearity between revenues and conference affiliation also complicates interpretation. Still, the experience of assembling data from multiple sources, diagnosing model issues, and adjusting methodology accordingly was valuable—even if the final product wasn't as clean as I'd hoped.
